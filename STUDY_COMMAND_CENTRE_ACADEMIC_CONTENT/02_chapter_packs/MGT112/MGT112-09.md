# MGT112 Chapter 9: Managing Work Groups and Work Teams

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter explains what groups are, how they develop, the major forces that shape group behavior, how groups differ from teams, what makes teams effective, and the challenges of global and virtual teamwork.

## 1. Groups and formal group types
- A group is two or more interacting and interdependent individuals who come together to achieve specific goals.
- Formal groups include command groups, task groups, cross-functional teams, and self-managed teams.
- Informal groups develop naturally around friendships and common interests.

## 2. Five stages of group development
- **Forming:** members join and define purpose, structure, and leadership amid uncertainty.
- **Storming:** intragroup conflict occurs over control and direction.
- **Norming:** close relationships, cohesiveness, identity, and shared expectations develop.
- **Performing:** the group structure functions and energy is directed toward the task.
- **Adjourning:** temporary groups prepare to disband.
- Groups do not always pass through the stages in a perfect sequence; the model is a general framework.

## 3. Major concepts of group behavior
- **Roles:** expected behavior patterns linked to a position. Role conflict occurs when expectations are incompatible.
- **Norms:** shared acceptable standards, including effort, performance, and dress.
- **Conformity:** pressure to align with the group. The Asch study showed that people may give an obviously wrong answer to conform.
- **Status:** prestige, position, or rank in a group.
- **Size:** small groups often complete tasks faster; large groups are useful for diverse input and fact finding.
- **Social loafing:** reduced individual effort when responsibility is dispersed.
- **Cohesiveness:** the degree to which members are attracted to one another and share goals. High cohesiveness improves productivity only when group goals support organizational goals.

## 4. Groups versus teams
- A work group shares information and makes decisions to help individuals perform their jobs.
- A work team works intensely on a common goal using positive synergy, complementary skills, and individual and mutual accountability.
- Teams are especially useful when tasks require multiple skills, judgment, experience, flexibility, and shared effort.

## 5. Types of teams
- Problem-solving teams improve work activities or solve specific problems.
- Self-managed work teams operate without a manager and control a complete work process or segment.
- Cross-functional teams combine people from different specialties.
- Virtual teams collaborate through digital technologies and may be more task oriented when members have not met.

## 6. Effective-team model
- **Context:** adequate resources, leadership and structure, trust, and suitable performance evaluation and rewards.
- **Composition:** abilities, personality, roles, diversity, size, flexibility, and preferences. Teams need technical, problem-solving/decision-making, and interpersonal skills.
- **Work design:** autonomy, skill variety, a whole identifiable task, and meaningful impact.
- **Processes:** common purpose, specific goals, team efficacy, useful task conflict, and limited social loafing.

## 7. Shaping and rewarding team behavior
- Select members with technical and interpersonal skills.
- Train employees in problem solving, communication, negotiation, conflict resolution, and coaching.
- Reward cooperation, information sharing, helping, conflict resolution, and learning needed skills.
- Teamwork also gives inherent rewards such as camaraderie and personal development.

## 8. Contemporary issues
- Global teams require attention to culture, language, conformity, status, social loafing, cohesiveness, conflict, and virtual communication.
- Teams are not always the answer. Use a team when the work benefits from multiple people, creates a shared purpose beyond individual goals, and contains genuine task interdependence.
