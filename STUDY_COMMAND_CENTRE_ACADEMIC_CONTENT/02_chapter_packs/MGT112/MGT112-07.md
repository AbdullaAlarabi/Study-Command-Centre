# MGT112 Chapter 7: Structuring and Designing Organizations

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter explains how managers divide, group, coordinate, and control work. It begins with the six elements of organizational structure, then compares mechanistic and organic organizations, explains the contingency factors that affect structure, and reviews traditional and contemporary designs.

## 1. Organizing and organizational design
- Organizing is the management function that creates the organization’s structure.
- Organizational design is the process of deciding how specialized jobs should be, what rules guide behavior, and where decisions will be made.
- The purpose of structure is to help work be completed efficiently and effectively.

## 2. Six elements of organizational structure
- **Work specialization:** dividing work activities into separate job tasks. It may improve efficiency, but excessive specialization can cause boredom, fatigue, stress, low quality, absenteeism, and turnover.
- **Departmentalization:** grouping jobs. The five common forms are functional, product, customer, geographic, and process.
- **Authority and responsibility:** authority is the right attached to a managerial position to give orders; responsibility is the obligation to perform assigned duties. Delegated authority should be matched with responsibility.
- **Span of control:** the number of employees a manager can efficiently and effectively direct. Training, task similarity, task complexity, proximity, procedures, information systems, values, and management style affect the appropriate span.
- **Centralization versus decentralization:** centralization places decisions at upper levels; decentralization gives lower-level managers input or decision authority.
- **Formalization:** the degree to which jobs are standardized and employee behavior is guided by rules and procedures.

## 3. Authority, power, and command
- The chain of command is the continuous line of authority from upper to lower levels.
- Line authority allows a manager to direct an employee’s work. Staff authority supports, assists, and advises line managers.
- Unity of command means each employee reports to one manager.
- Authority comes from position; power is the capacity to influence decisions and does not require a managerial title.
- The five bases of power are coercive, reward, legitimate, expert, and referent.

## 4. Mechanistic and organic models
- **Mechanistic:** rigid hierarchy, fixed duties, many rules, formal communication, centralized authority, narrow spans, taller structure.
- **Organic:** vertical and horizontal collaboration, adaptable duties, few rules, informal communication, decentralized authority, wide spans, flatter structure.

## 5. Contingency factors affecting structure
- **Strategy:** simple strategies usually fit simple structures; innovation fits organic designs; cost control fits mechanistic designs.
- **Size:** large organizations, especially above about 2,000 employees, tend to become more mechanistic.
- **Technology:** the way inputs are converted to outputs influences the appropriate structure.
- **Environment:** stable environments favor mechanistic structures; dynamic and uncertain environments favor organic structures.

## 6. Traditional designs
- **Simple structure:** low departmentalization, wide spans, centralized authority, little formalization. Fast and inexpensive, but can overload the owner or top manager as the firm grows.
- **Functional structure:** groups similar occupational specialties. It gains economies of scale and reduces duplication, but functions may become isolated and lose sight of total organizational results.
- **Divisional structure:** separate business units or divisions with accountable managers. It focuses on results but duplicates activities and resources.

## 7. Contemporary designs
- **Team structure:** the organization is made up of work teams; empowerment is essential.
- **Matrix structure:** specialists from functional departments work on projects and report to both a functional manager and a project manager. It improves coordination but can create confusion and power struggles.
- **Project structure:** employees continuously move from one project to another and do not return to permanent functional departments.
- **Boundaryless organization:** reduces internal and external boundaries. Virtual organizations use a small core plus temporary specialists; network organizations perform some work internally and outsource other activities.

## 8. Current design challenges
- Keep remote and dispersed employees connected.
- Recognize cultural differences in global structures.
- Build a learning organization in which employees acquire, share, and apply knowledge.
- Use flexible arrangements such as remote work, telecommuting, compressed workweeks, flextime, job sharing, and contingent work.
