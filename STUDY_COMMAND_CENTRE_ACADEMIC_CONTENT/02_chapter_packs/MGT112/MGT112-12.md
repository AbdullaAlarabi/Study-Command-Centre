# MGT112 Chapter 12: Understanding Leadership

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter moves from early trait and behavioral approaches to four contingency theories, then examines modern leadership views, empowerment, culture, emotional intelligence, and trust.

## 1. Leader and leadership
- A leader is someone who can influence others and who has managerial authority.
- Leadership is the process of leading a group and influencing it to achieve its goals.

## 2. Trait and behavioral theories
- Trait theories searched for characteristics that distinguish leaders from nonleaders. No universal set was found.
- Seven traits associated with leadership are drive, desire to lead, honesty and integrity, self-confidence, intelligence, job-relevant knowledge, and extraversion.
- University of Iowa styles: democratic, autocratic, and laissez-faire.
- Ohio State dimensions: consideration and initiating structure.
- Michigan dimensions: employee oriented and production oriented.
- The Managerial Grid suggests high concern for people and high concern for production, the 9,9 style.

## 3. Fiedler contingency model
- Fiedler uses the least-preferred coworker questionnaire to identify task-oriented or relationship-oriented style.
- Situational favorableness depends on leader-member relations, task structure, and position power.
- Task-oriented leaders perform best in very favorable or very unfavorable situations. Relationship-oriented leaders perform best in moderately favorable situations.
- Fiedler treats leadership style as relatively fixed.

## 4. Situational leadership theory
- Hersey and Blanchard focus on follower readiness: ability and willingness to perform a task.
- Telling: high task, low relationship.
- Selling: high task, high relationship.
- Participating: low task, high relationship.
- Delegating: low task, low relationship.
- Readiness moves from R1 unable/unwilling, through R2 unable/willing and R3 able/unwilling, to R4 able/willing.

## 5. Leader-participation and path-goal theories
- The leader-participation model uses a decision tree and contingency rules to determine the appropriate amount of participation in decision making.
- Path-goal theory says the leader helps followers reach goals and removes obstacles.
- Path-goal behaviors are directive, supportive, participative, and achievement oriented.
- The best behavior depends on environmental factors and follower characteristics.

## 6. Modern leadership views
- LMX theory says leaders form in-groups and out-groups; in-group members generally receive higher ratings, lower turnover, and greater satisfaction.
- Transactional leaders use exchanges and rewards. Transformational leaders inspire extraordinary outcomes and encourage followers to question established views.
- Charismatic leaders are enthusiastic and self-confident, articulate a vision, accept risk, understand constraints and followers, and display unusual behavior.
- Visionary leadership creates and communicates a realistic, credible, and attractive future.

## 7. Teams, empowerment, culture, and EI
- Team leaders manage the team’s external boundary and facilitate team processes through coaching, communication, training, and performance support.
- Empowerment increases employees’ decision-making discretion.
- National culture affects the leadership style followers expect, although vision, encouragement, trustworthiness, dynamism, positivity, and proactivity have broad value.
- Emotional intelligence supports leadership through self-awareness, self-management, self-motivation, empathy, and social skills.

## 8. Trust
- Trust is belief in the integrity, character, and ability of a leader.
- Its five dimensions are integrity, competence, consistency, loyalty, and openness.
- Leaders build trust through openness, fairness, truthfulness, consistency, promises, confidentiality, and confidence.
- Experience, professional orientation, routine jobs, formal rules, or cohesive groups can sometimes substitute for leadership.
