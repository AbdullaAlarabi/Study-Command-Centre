# MGT112 Chapter 10: Understanding Individual Behavior

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter introduces organizational behavior and explains how attitudes, personality, perception, and learning influence employee behavior. It also covers attribution errors, perceptual shortcuts, behavior-shaping methods, and current workplace issues.

## 1. Organizational behavior and its goals
- Organizational behavior is the study of the actions of people at work.
- It focuses on individual behavior, group behavior, and organizational systems such as structure, culture, and HR practices.
- Its goals are to explain, predict, and influence behavior.
- Key employee outcomes are productivity, absenteeism, turnover, organizational citizenship behavior, job satisfaction, and workplace misbehavior.

## 2. Attitudes
- An attitude is an evaluative statement about objects, people, or events.
- Its components are cognitive, affective, and behavioral.
- Important job attitudes are job satisfaction, job involvement, organizational commitment, and employee engagement.
- Cognitive dissonance is an inconsistency between attitudes or between attitudes and behavior. People try to reduce it depending on importance, control, and rewards.
- Satisfied and committed employees generally show lower turnover and absenteeism.

## 3. Personality frameworks
- Personality is the unique combination of emotional, thought, and behavioral patterns that affects reactions and interactions.
- The MBTI uses four dimensions: Extraversion–Introversion, Sensing–Intuition, Thinking–Feeling, and Judging–Perceiving.
- The Big Five are extraversion, agreeableness, conscientiousness, emotional stability, and openness to experience.
- Conscientiousness predicts performance across many occupations; other traits matter depending on the job.

## 4. Emotional intelligence and work-related traits
- Emotional intelligence includes self-awareness, self-management, self-motivation, empathy, and social skills.
- Five useful personality traits are locus of control, Machiavellianism, self-esteem, self-monitoring, and risk propensity.
- Holland’s personality-job fit theory argues that satisfaction and turnover depend partly on the fit between personality and job environment.

## 5. Perception and attribution
- Perception is the process of organizing and interpreting sensory impressions to give meaning to the environment.
- Attribution theory explains whether behavior is judged as internally or externally caused using distinctiveness, consensus, and consistency.
- The fundamental attribution error is overestimating internal causes and underestimating external causes when judging others.
- Self-serving bias means crediting personal success to internal factors and blaming failure on external factors.

## 6. Perceptual shortcuts
- Selective perception filters information based on interests, background, experience, and attitudes.
- Assumed similarity judges others through the observer’s own characteristics.
- Stereotyping judges a person according to the group to which the person is perceived to belong.
- The halo effect forms an overall impression from one characteristic.

## 7. Learning and shaping behavior
- Learning is a relatively permanent change in behavior resulting from experience.
- Operant conditioning says behavior is a function of its consequences.
- Social learning occurs through observation and direct experience and depends on attention, retention, motor reproduction, and reinforcement.
- Behavior can be shaped through positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement, punishment, and extinction.
- Managers must watch both what they reward and the behavior they model.

## 8. Contemporary issues
- Different generations may differ in communication, technology use, work-life priorities, and preferred leadership styles.
- Managers must respond to negative workplace behavior and create fair, respectful working conditions.
