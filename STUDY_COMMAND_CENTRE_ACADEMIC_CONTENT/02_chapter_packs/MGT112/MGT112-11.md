# MGT112 Chapter 11: Motivating and Rewarding Employees

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter defines motivation, compares early and contemporary motivation theories, explains job design and fairness, and shows how managers can motivate different workers through suitable goals, jobs, communication, recognition, and rewards.

## 1. Motivation
- Motivation is the process by which a person’s efforts are energized, directed, and sustained toward attaining a goal.
- The three elements are energy, direction toward organizational goals, and persistence.

## 2. Maslow and McGregor
- Maslow’s needs are physiological, safety, social, esteem, and self-actualization. A substantially satisfied need stops being a strong motivator. The theory is intuitive but lacks strong empirical support.
- Theory X assumes employees dislike work, avoid responsibility, have little ambition, and need close control.
- Theory Y assumes employees can enjoy work, accept responsibility, and exercise self-direction.

## 3. Herzberg and McClelland
- Herzberg’s two-factor theory separates motivators from hygiene factors. Intrinsic factors such as achievement, recognition, and responsibility create satisfaction; extrinsic job-context factors such as policy, supervision, relationships, and working conditions create dissatisfaction.
- The opposite of satisfaction is no satisfaction; the opposite of dissatisfaction is no dissatisfaction.
- McClelland’s acquired needs are achievement, power, and affiliation. High achievers prefer personal responsibility, clear feedback, and moderately challenging goals.

## 4. Goal-setting theory
- Specific goals increase performance, and difficult goals generally produce higher performance than easy goals.
- Feedback helps people identify the gap between actual and desired performance.
- Goal commitment, self-efficacy, and national culture affect the goal-performance relationship.

## 5. Job characteristics model
- The five core job dimensions are skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, and feedback.
- Skill variety, task identity, and task significance create experienced meaningfulness.
- Autonomy creates responsibility for outcomes; feedback creates knowledge of results.
- Job enrichment vertically expands a job by adding planning and evaluation responsibilities.

## 6. Equity theory
- Employees compare their outcomes-to-inputs ratio with a relevant other.
- Perceived inequity can change effort, output, quality, absenteeism, or turnover.
- Referents may be other persons, organizational systems, or the employee’s past self.
- Distributive justice concerns the fairness of reward amounts; procedural justice concerns the fairness of the process used to allocate rewards.

## 7. Expectancy theory
- Expectancy theory says action depends on the expectation of an outcome and the attractiveness of that outcome.
- **Expectancy:** effort will lead to performance.
- **Instrumentality:** performance will lead to a reward.
- **Valence:** the reward is valuable to the individual.
- Motivation is strongest when all three links are strong.

## 8. Current issues and rewards
- Motivation practices must consider national culture and the needs of diverse employees, professionals, and contingent workers.
- Open-book management shares financial information so employees understand the business impact of their decisions.
- Recognition programs provide attention and appreciation for good work.
- Pay-for-performance links compensation to individual, team, department, or organizational results.
- Low-cost motivation includes clear roles, open communication, employee input, shared goals, community, and learning opportunities.
