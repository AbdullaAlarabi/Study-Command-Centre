# MGT112 Chapter 8: Managing Human Resources and Diversity

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter covers the full human resource management process: planning for employees, recruiting, selecting, orienting, training, evaluating, compensating, and retaining them. It also addresses downsizing, changing workforces, harassment, diversity, inclusion, and legal influences.

## 1. Human resource management
- Human resource management is the management function concerned with getting, training, motivating, and keeping competent employees.
- The goal is to place the right people in the right place at the right time.

## 2. The HRM process and its influences
- The eight key activities are employment planning, recruitment, downsizing, selection, orientation, training and development, performance appraisal, and compensation and benefits.
- The process is influenced by the external environment, especially laws, economic conditions, technology, and social expectations.
- Affirmative action programs seek to improve the employment, upgrading, and retention of protected groups.

## 3. Employment planning and job analysis
- Employment planning ensures the organization has the right number and kinds of people in the right places at the right times.
- A human resource inventory records employee education, training, experience, languages, capabilities, and specialized skills.
- Job analysis identifies workflows and the skills and behaviors needed to perform jobs.
- A job description states what a job holder does, how it is done, and why it is done.
- A job specification states the minimum knowledge, skills, and attitudes required for successful performance.

## 4. Recruitment, downsizing, and selection
- Recruitment is locating, identifying, and attracting capable applicants.
- If staffing is excessive, managers may use downsizing or other restructuring options.
- Selection predicts which applicants will succeed if hired.
- An accept error occurs when an applicant is hired but performs poorly. A reject error occurs when a capable applicant is rejected.
- Reliability means a selection device measures consistently. Validity means it has a proven relationship with a relevant job-performance criterion.

## 5. Selection devices and interviews
- Selection tools include written tests, performance-simulation tests, and interviews.
- Work sampling is a miniature replica of a routine job; assessment centers simulate managerial problems.
- Interviews are more useful when structured, standardized, job-related, and consistently evaluated.
- A realistic job preview gives both positive and negative information, helping reduce later dissatisfaction and turnover.

## 6. Orientation and training
- Orientation reduces anxiety, explains duties, introduces the work unit, and explains the organization’s goals, rules, policies, and procedures.
- Training seeks a relatively permanent improvement in job knowledge, skills, or attitudes.
- Training needs may be indicated by falling output, low quality, accidents, rejection rates, job redesign, or new technology.
- Training evaluation should consider reaction, learning, use of skills on the job, and actual results.

## 7. Performance and compensation
- A performance management system establishes standards used to evaluate employee performance.
- Multi-person comparisons include group-order ranking, individual ranking, and paired comparison.
- Poor performance may result from job mismatch, inadequate training, or discipline problems; the response should match the cause.
- Compensation should be cost effective and perceived as fair. Pay may be skill based or variable and may include nonfinancial benefits.

## 8. Contemporary HR issues and diversity
- Downsizing is the planned elimination of jobs. Survivors may experience insecurity, unfairness, guilt, stress, reduced loyalty, and lower effort.
- Sexual harassment is unwanted conduct of a sexual nature that affects employment, performance, or the work environment.
- Managers must adapt to work-life needs and contingent workers.
- Workforce diversity means the ways people are different from and similar to one another. Inclusion must be supported throughout recruitment, selection, orientation, training, performance management, and compensation.
