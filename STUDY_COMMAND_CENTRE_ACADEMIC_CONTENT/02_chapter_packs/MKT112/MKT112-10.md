# MKT112 Chapter 10: Pricing: Understanding and Capturing Customer Value

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter explains what price is, why it matters, the three major pricing strategies, cost and demand concepts, market structures, price elasticity, and the internal and external factors that influence pricing decisions.

## 1. Meaning and importance of price
- Price is the amount charged for a product or service, or the total value customers exchange for the benefits of using it.
- Price is the only marketing-mix element that produces revenue; the other elements represent costs.
- Price affects market share, profitability, positioning, customer value, and relationships and can be changed quickly.

## 2. The pricing range and three strategies
- Customer-perceived value sets the price ceiling: above it, customers will not buy.
- Product cost sets the price floor: below it, the firm cannot earn profit.
- Between them, the company considers competition, strategy, objectives, mix, market, and demand.
- The three major strategies are customer value-based pricing, cost-based pricing, and competition-based pricing.

## 3. Customer value-based pricing
- Value-based pricing starts with buyers’ perceptions of value and sets a price that captures that value.
- Cost-based pricing is product driven; value-based pricing is customer driven.
- Good-value pricing offers the right combination of quality and service at a fair price.
- Everyday low pricing uses constant low prices with few promotions; high-low pricing uses regular higher prices with frequent temporary discounts.
- Value-added pricing attaches differentiating features and services to support higher prices.

## 4. Cost-based pricing
- Cost-based pricing adds a fair return to the costs of producing, distributing, and selling the product.
- Fixed costs do not vary with output; variable costs change directly with production; total cost is fixed plus variable costs.
- Cost-plus pricing adds a standard markup. It is simple and can appear fair, but it ignores demand and competitor prices.
- Break-even or target-return pricing identifies a price and volume at which total revenue equals total cost or reaches a target return.

## 5. Competition-based pricing
- Competition-based pricing uses competitors’ strategies, costs, prices, and market offers.
- The firm asks how its customer value compares with competitors and how strong competitors are.

## 6. Internal factors
- Pricing must fit the overall marketing strategy, objectives, positioning, product, distribution, and promotion.
- Target costing begins with an ideal selling price based on customer value and then determines the costs that can be incurred.
- Top management normally sets objectives and policies, while sales, production, finance, accounting, and specialist pricing departments may influence decisions.

## 7. Market structures and demand
- Pure competition has many buyers and sellers trading a uniform commodity.
- Monopolistic competition has many buyers and sellers with differentiated offers and a range of prices.
- Oligopolistic competition has only a few large sellers who closely monitor one another.
- Pure monopoly has one dominant seller.
- A demand curve shows the quantity buyers will purchase at different prices; normally, price and demand are inversely related.

## 8. Elasticity and other external factors
- Price elasticity measures how sensitive demand is to a price change.
- Inelastic demand changes little after a small price change; elastic demand changes greatly.
- Economic conditions, inflation, interest rates, consumer spending, reseller reactions, government action, and social concerns influence price.
- Even in difficult economic conditions, companies should protect customer value rather than automatically cut price and weaken the brand.
