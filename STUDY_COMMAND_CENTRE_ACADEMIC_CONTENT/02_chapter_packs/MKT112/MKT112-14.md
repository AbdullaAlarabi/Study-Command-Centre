# MKT112 Chapter 14: Integrated Marketing Communications Strategy

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter explains the promotion mix, why integrated marketing communications is necessary, the communication process, the steps in developing effective communication, promotion-budget methods, push and pull strategies, and responsible communication.

## 1. Promotion mix
- The promotion mix is the blend of tools used to communicate customer value and build relationships.
- Advertising is paid, nonpersonal presentation and promotion by an identified sponsor.
- Sales promotion uses short-term incentives such as discounts, coupons, displays, demonstrations, and events.
- Personal selling is direct interaction by the sales force to engage customers, sell, and build relationships.
- Public relations builds good relations with publics, obtains favorable publicity, builds image, and handles rumors or events.
- Direct and digital marketing engages targeted customers immediately and interactively.

## 2. Why integrated marketing communications is needed
- Consumers are better informed and use their own digital sources.
- Mass markets have fragmented into smaller micromarkets.
- Digital tools have multiplied customer touch points and communication channels.
- Integrated marketing communications coordinates channels to deliver clear, consistent, and compelling company and brand messages.
- Without integration, customers receive conflicting content and confused positioning.

## 3. Nine communication-process elements
- Sender, encoding, message, media, decoding, receiver, response, feedback, and noise.
- Effective communication requires overlap between the sender’s and receiver’s fields of experience.
- Noise is unplanned distortion that causes the receiver to obtain a message different from the one intended.

## 4. Developing effective communication
- Identify the target audience.
- Determine communication objectives.
- Design the message.
- Choose communication channels and media.
- Select a credible message source.
- Collect feedback and measure awareness, recall, attitudes, purchasing, discussion, and store visits.

## 5. Target audience, objectives, and message design
- The target audience can include users, potential buyers, decision makers, influencers, groups, special publics, or the general public.
- Communication objectives may move customers through awareness, knowledge, liking, preference, conviction, and purchase.
- Message design decides what to say and how to say it, including content, structure, and presentation.

## 6. Personal and nonpersonal channels
- Personal communication includes face-to-face, phone, mail/email, text, chat, experts, advocates, bloggers, friends, family, and associates.
- Buzz marketing cultivates opinion leaders who spread product information.
- Nonpersonal channels include major media, atmospheres, and events and carry messages without personal contact or feedback.

## 7. Promotion budgets and mix
- Affordable method: spend what management believes the company can afford.
- Percentage-of-sales method: use a percentage of actual or forecast sales or unit price.
- Competitive-parity method: match competitors’ promotion spending.
- Objective-and-task method: define objectives, identify tasks, and estimate their costs.
- The promotion tools should be blended according to their different strengths and limitations.

## 8. Push, pull, integration, and responsibility
- A push strategy directs personal selling and trade promotion at channel members to move the product toward consumers.
- A pull strategy directs advertising and consumer promotion at final consumers so their demand pulls the product through the channel.
- All promotional tools and customer touch points should reinforce the same positioning.
- Communication must avoid false or deceptive claims, bait-and-switch tactics, bribery, theft of trade secrets, and false disparagement of competitors.
