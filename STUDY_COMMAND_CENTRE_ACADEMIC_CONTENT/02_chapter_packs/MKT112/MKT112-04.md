# MKT112 Chapter 4: Managing Marketing Information to Gain Customer Insights

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter explains how marketers turn data into customer insight. It covers the marketing information ecosystem, internal data, competitive intelligence, the four-step marketing research process, primary and secondary data, research methods, sampling, analytics, and artificial intelligence.

## 1. Customer insights, data, and the marketing information ecosystem
- Customer insights are fresh, deep understandings of what customers need and want, developed from good marketing information.
- Big data consists of huge and complex data sets generated by modern information, storage, and analysis technologies.
- Marketers often need better information and better use of existing information, not simply more data.
- A marketing information ecosystem consists of the people, processes, and assets used to assess information needs, develop information, and apply it to decisions.

## 2. The marketing information flow
- The system begins with information users, assesses their needs, develops information, and helps them analyze and apply it.
- Information is developed from internal databases, competitive marketing intelligence, and primary or secondary marketing research.
- A good system balances what managers want, what they truly need, what is feasible, and whether the insight is worth its cost.

## 3. Internal data and competitive marketing intelligence
- Internal databases contain information such as customer characteristics, sales, web visits, service records, costs, cash flows, production, shipments, inventories, reseller reactions, competitor activity, and point-of-sale transactions.
- Internal data is usually fast and inexpensive but may be incomplete, outdated, or in the wrong form.
- Competitive marketing intelligence is the systematic collection and analysis of publicly available information about consumers, competitors, and the marketing environment.
- Its purpose is to improve decisions, understand the environment, track competitors, and give early warning of opportunities and threats.
- Intelligence gathering must remain ethical.

## 4. Marketing research and its four steps
- Marketing research is the systematic design, collection, analysis, and reporting of data relevant to a specific marketing situation.
- The four steps are: define the problem and objectives; develop the research plan; implement the plan and collect/analyze data; interpret and report findings.
- The first step is especially important because an incorrectly defined problem makes the entire study ineffective.

## 5. Research objectives and planning
- Exploratory research gathers preliminary information and suggests hypotheses.
- Descriptive research describes markets, situations, consumer characteristics, attitudes, or market potential.
- Causal research tests cause-and-effect relationships.
- The research plan specifies information needs, existing-data sources, research approaches, contact methods, sampling, instruments, cost, and intended managerial use.

## 6. Secondary and primary data
- Secondary data already exists and was collected for another purpose. It is cheaper and faster but may lack relevance, accuracy, currency, or impartiality.
- Primary data is collected for the specific research purpose.
- Primary-data planning requires decisions about research approaches, contact methods, sampling, and research instruments.

## 7. Primary research approaches and contact methods
- Observational research studies actual consumer behavior. Ethnographic research observes and interacts with consumers in natural settings; netnography does this online.
- Survey research asks about knowledge, attitudes, preferences, and behavior and is suited to descriptive information.
- Experimental research uses matched groups, different treatments, controls, and response comparisons to study causal relationships.
- Contact methods include mail, telephone, personal interviews, focus groups, and online/mobile methods. Each has cost, speed, flexibility, honesty, response-rate, and bias trade-offs.

## 8. Sampling, implementation, analytics, and AI
- A sample is a segment selected to represent the population. Researchers decide who, how many, and how participants will be chosen.
- Implementation includes collecting, processing, analyzing, interpreting, and reporting information.
- Managers and researchers should interpret findings together and focus on decision-useful insights rather than overwhelming statistics.
- Marketing analytics uses tools, technologies, and processes to find meaningful patterns in big data.
- Artificial intelligence lets machines think and learn in a human-like way with greater analytical capacity. It can personalize customer experiences, but managers must avoid losing the big picture or relying blindly on data.
