# MKT112 Chapter 18: Creating Competitive Advantage

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter explains how firms identify, assess, and select competitors; build a competitive intelligence system; choose value disciplines and market-position strategies; and balance customer and competitor orientations.

## 1. Competitive advantage and competitor analysis
- Competitive advantage requires delivering more value and satisfaction to target customers than competitors.
- Competitor analysis is the process of identifying, assessing, and selecting key competitors.
- Competitive marketing strategies position a company against competitors through superior customer value.

## 2. Identifying competitors
- Competitors include firms making the same product or product class, firms satisfying the same need, and firms competing for the same customer money.
- A market-based view normally identifies a broader and more useful set of competitors than a narrow product view.

## 3. Assessing competitors
- Study competitor objectives such as profitability, market share, cash flow, technology leadership, or service leadership.
- A strategic group contains firms following the same or a similar strategy in a target market. Competition is usually strongest within the group.
- Assess strengths and weaknesses using secondary data, experience, word of mouth, research, websites, and social platforms.
- Benchmarking compares products and processes with competitors or leading firms to identify best practices.
- Estimate how each competitor is likely to react to price cuts, promotion, or product changes.

## 4. Selecting competitors and intelligence systems
- Customer value analysis identifies the attributes customers value and compares the company and competitors on those attributes.
- Companies may choose strong or weak, close or distant, and good or bad competitors.
- A competitive intelligence system identifies sources, continually collects information, checks validity and reliability, interprets and organizes it, distributes it to decision makers, and answers inquiries.

## 5. Basic competitive strategies
- Michael Porter’s broad approaches include overall cost leadership, differentiation, and focus.
- Value disciplines include operational excellence, customer intimacy, and product leadership.
- Operational excellence delivers superior value through price and convenience.
- Customer intimacy uses close customer knowledge and responsive relationships.
- Product leadership delivers a stream of leading-edge products or services.

## 6. Competitive market positions
- Market leader: largest market share.
- Market challenger: runner-up fighting to increase share.
- Market follower: runner-up seeking to hold share without provoking a major fight.
- Market nicher: serves small segments not pursued by major firms.

## 7. Strategies by position
- Leaders expand total demand through new users, new uses, and more usage; protect share by correcting weaknesses, maintaining value, relationships, and innovation; and expand share carefully when the return exceeds the cost.
- Challengers can attack the leader or smaller firms using direct or indirect strategies and may use a second-mover advantage by improving on the leader.
- Followers copy or improve successful programs with lower investment while adding distinct value and controlling costs.
- Nichers specialize by market, customer, product, service, quality, price, or marketing mix in segments that are profitable and unattractive to larger firms.

## 8. Balancing orientations
- A competitor-centered company tracks rivals closely and fights effectively but can become reactive.
- A customer-centered company monitors customer needs and is better positioned to identify long-term opportunities.
- A market-centered company balances customer and competitor understanding.
- Product orientation ignores both; market orientation is the desired combination of customer and competitor focus.
