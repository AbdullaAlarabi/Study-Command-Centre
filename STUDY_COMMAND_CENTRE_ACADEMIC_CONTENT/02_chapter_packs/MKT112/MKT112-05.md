# MKT112 Chapter 5: Consumer Markets and Buyer Behavior

## Chapter in one minute
This chapter explains the consumer buying model, the cultural, social, personal, and psychological forces that influence consumers, the major types of buying behavior, the five-stage buying process, the customer journey, and the adoption of new products.

## 1. Consumer buyer behavior and the black box
- Consumer buyer behavior is the buying behavior of final consumers who buy goods and services for personal consumption.
- Consumer markets include all individuals and households buying or acquiring goods and services for personal consumption.
- Marketing and other environmental stimuli enter the buyer’s “black box,” which contains buyer characteristics and the decision process, and produce attitudes, preferences, purchases, and brand relationships.

## 2. Cultural influences
- Culture is the set of basic values, perceptions, wants, and behaviors learned from family and important institutions.
- A subculture is a group within a culture that shares values based on common experiences and situations.
- Marketers monitor cultural shifts because they can create demand for new products and services.

## 3. Social and personal influences
- Social influences include groups and social networks, word of mouth, opinion leaders, family, roles, and status.
- Family is the most important consumer-buying organization in society.
- Personal influences include age and life stage, occupation, economic situation, lifestyle, personality, and self-concept.

## 4. Psychological influences
- A motive is a need strong enough to direct a person to seek satisfaction.
- Maslow’s hierarchy moves from physiological and safety needs to social, esteem, and self-actualization needs.
- Perception is the process of selecting, organizing, and interpreting information.
- Selective attention screens information; selective distortion interprets it to support existing beliefs; selective retention remembers favorable information about preferred brands.
- Learning changes behavior through drives, stimuli, cues, responses, and reinforcement.
- Beliefs are descriptive thoughts; attitudes are relatively consistent evaluations, feelings, and tendencies.

## 5. Four types of buying behavior
- **Complex buying:** high involvement and significant brand differences.
- **Dissonance-reducing buying:** high involvement but few perceived brand differences.
- **Habitual buying:** low involvement and few significant brand differences.
- **Variety-seeking buying:** low involvement but significant brand differences.

## 6. Buyer decision process
- **Need recognition:** an internal or external stimulus creates awareness of a problem or need.
- **Information search:** the consumer looks for information from personal, commercial, public, and experiential sources.
- **Evaluation of alternatives:** the consumer evaluates brands in the choice set.
- **Purchase decision:** the consumer chooses a brand, although other people’s attitudes or unexpected situations may change the decision.
- **Postpurchase behavior:** satisfaction or dissatisfaction depends on the relationship between expectations and perceived performance.

## 7. Postpurchase behavior and customer journey
- Cognitive dissonance is buyer discomfort caused by postpurchase conflict.
- Marketers should reinforce the chosen brand’s benefits and aim to delight customers.
- The customer journey is the sum of ongoing brand experiences that shape buying, engagement, and advocacy over time.

## 8. Adoption of new products
- The adoption stages are awareness, interest, evaluation, trial, and adoption.
- Adopter groups are innovators, early adopters, early mainstream, late mainstream, and lagging adopters.
- Rate of adoption is influenced by relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability, as well as cost, risk, uncertainty, and social approval.
