# Chapter 4: Managing Marketing Information to Gain Customer Insights

## Rapid review
Customer insights are fresh, deep understandings of what customers need and want. The marketing information ecosystem contains the people, processes, and assets used to assess information needs, develop information, and help managers apply it.

### Main information sources
- Internal databases
- Competitive marketing intelligence
- Primary and secondary marketing research

**Internal data** is fast and inexpensive but may be incomplete, outdated, or unsuitable. **Competitive marketing intelligence** systematically collects and analyzes publicly available information about consumers, competitors, and the environment and must remain ethical.

### Four-step research process
1. Define the problem and research objectives.
2. Develop the research plan.
3. Implement the plan and collect/analyze data.
4. Interpret and report findings.

### Research objectives
- **Exploratory:** gather preliminary information and suggest hypotheses.
- **Descriptive:** describe markets, customers, attitudes, or potential.
- **Causal:** test cause-and-effect relationships.

### Data and research methods
- **Secondary data:** already exists and was collected for another purpose.
- **Primary data:** collected for the current research purpose.
- Observation studies behavior.
- Survey collects knowledge, attitudes, preferences, and behavior.
- Experiment studies causal relationships.
- Sampling asks: who, how many, and how to select.
- Contact methods include mail, phone, personal interviews, focus groups, and online/mobile methods.

### Analytics and AI
Marketing analytics searches data for useful patterns. AI can improve analysis and personalization, but managers should not rely blindly on data or lose the wider business picture.

## Must remember
- Information ecosystem
- Three information sources
- Four research steps
- Exploratory, descriptive, causal
- Primary versus secondary
- Observation, survey, experiment
- Three sampling decisions

# Chapter 5: Consumer Markets and Buyer Behavior

## Rapid review
Consumer buyer behavior is the buying behavior of individuals and households purchasing for personal use. Marketing and environmental stimuli enter the buyer’s black box and produce attitudes, preferences, purchases, and relationships.

### Four influence groups
1. **Cultural:** culture and subculture.
2. **Social:** groups, networks, word of mouth, opinion leaders, family, roles, status.
3. **Personal:** age, life stage, occupation, economic situation, lifestyle, personality, self-concept.
4. **Psychological:** motivation, perception, learning, beliefs, attitudes.

### Perception
- Selective attention
- Selective distortion
- Selective retention

### Four types of buying behavior
- Complex: high involvement, major brand differences.
- Dissonance-reducing: high involvement, few brand differences.
- Habitual: low involvement, few brand differences.
- Variety-seeking: low involvement, major brand differences.

### Five-stage buyer decision process
1. Need recognition
2. Information search
3. Evaluation of alternatives
4. Purchase decision
5. Postpurchase behavior

Satisfaction depends on the relationship between expectations and perceived performance. Cognitive dissonance is postpurchase discomfort or conflict.

### Adoption of new products
- Stages: awareness, interest, evaluation, trial, adoption.
- Adopter groups: innovators, early adopters, early mainstream, late mainstream, lagging adopters.
- Product characteristics: relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, observability.

## Must remember
- Black-box model
- Four influence groups
- Four buying types
- Five decision stages
- Five adoption stages
- Five adopter groups
- Five product characteristics

# Chapter 10: Pricing and Customer Value

## Rapid review
Price is the money charged or the value customers exchange for the benefits of a product or service. It is the only marketing-mix element that produces revenue.

### Price floor and ceiling
- Customer-perceived value sets the **ceiling**.
- Product cost sets the **floor**.
- Between them, the firm considers competitors, objectives, strategy, market, and demand.

### Three pricing strategies
- **Customer value-based:** begins with buyer perceptions of value.
- **Cost-based:** begins with production, distribution, and selling costs plus a return.
- **Competition-based:** considers competitors’ strategies, costs, prices, and offers.

### Value pricing forms
- Good-value pricing
- Everyday low pricing (EDLP)
- High-low pricing
- Value-added pricing

### Costs and cost-based methods
- Fixed cost does not vary with output.
- Variable cost varies with output.
- Total cost equals fixed plus variable cost.
- Cost-plus pricing adds a standard markup.
- Break-even or target-return pricing calculates the volume/price needed to cover cost or achieve a target return.

### Other factors
- Target costing starts with an ideal selling price and works backward to allowable cost.
- Market structures: pure competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, pure monopoly.
- Price elasticity measures demand sensitivity to price changes.
- Economic conditions, resellers, government, and social concerns affect pricing.

## Must remember
- Price definition and importance
- Floor and ceiling
- Three pricing strategies
- EDLP versus high-low
- Fixed, variable, total cost
- Cost-plus and break-even
- Four market structures
- Elastic versus inelastic demand

# High-value comparison table

| Pair | Key distinction |
|---|---|
| Internal data / Marketing intelligence | Company records / Public information about market and competitors |
| Primary / Secondary data | Collected for current purpose / Already exists |
| Exploratory / Descriptive / Causal | Preliminary understanding / Description / Cause-and-effect test |
| Observation / Survey / Experiment | Watch behavior / Ask respondents / Manipulate treatments |
| Complex / Dissonance-reducing | High involvement + many differences / High involvement + few differences |
| Habitual / Variety-seeking | Low involvement + few differences / Low involvement + many differences |
| Selective attention / distortion / retention | Notice / Interpret / Remember selectively |
| Value-based / Cost-based | Buyer value first / Product cost first |
| EDLP / High-low | Constant low prices / Higher regular prices with promotions |
| Elastic / Inelastic demand | Large demand response / Small demand response |
