# Chapter 7: Structuring and Designing Organizations

## Rapid review
Organizing is the management function that creates an organization’s structure. Organizational design is the process of deciding how jobs are divided, how employees are grouped, who has authority, how many employees report to each manager, where decisions are made, and how strongly work is controlled by rules.

### Six elements of organizational structure
1. **Work specialization:** dividing work activities into separate job tasks. It can improve efficiency, but excessive specialization can cause boredom, fatigue, stress, low quality, absenteeism, and turnover.
2. **Departmentalization:** grouping jobs. The five common forms are functional, product, customer, geographic, and process.
3. **Authority and responsibility:** authority is the right attached to a position to give orders; responsibility is the obligation to perform assigned duties.
4. **Span of control:** the number of employees a manager can efficiently and effectively direct.
5. **Centralization versus decentralization:** centralization keeps decisions at upper levels; decentralization gives lower-level managers input or authority.
6. **Formalization:** the degree to which jobs are standardized and guided by rules and procedures.

### Authority, command, and power
- **Chain of command:** continuous line of authority from upper to lower levels.
- **Unity of command:** each employee should report to one manager.
- **Line authority:** direct authority over an employee’s work.
- **Staff authority:** authority to support, assist, or advise line managers.
- **Power:** the capacity to influence decisions.
- Five types of power: **coercive, reward, legitimate, expert, referent**.

### Mechanistic versus organic designs
| Mechanistic | Organic |
|---|---|
| Rigid and controlled | Adaptive and flexible |
| High specialization | Collaboration and adaptable duties |
| Many rules | Few rules |
| Formal communication | Informal communication |
| Centralized authority | Decentralized authority |
| Narrow spans and taller structure | Wider spans and flatter structure |

### Contingency factors
- **Strategy:** innovation usually fits organic design; cost control usually fits mechanistic design.
- **Size:** larger organizations tend to become more mechanistic.
- **Technology:** structure should fit how inputs are converted into outputs.
- **Environment:** stability favors mechanistic design; uncertainty favors organic design.

### Main structures
- **Simple:** low departmentalization, wide spans, centralized authority, little formalization.
- **Functional:** groups similar occupational specialties.
- **Divisional:** separate units with accountable managers.
- **Team:** organization is made up of work teams.
- **Matrix:** specialists report to both functional and project managers.
- **Project:** employees continuously move between projects.
- **Boundaryless:** reduces internal and external boundaries; may be virtual or network based.

## Must remember
- Six structural elements
- Five forms of departmentalization
- Five types of power
- Mechanistic versus organic
- Four contingency factors
- Simple, functional, divisional, team, matrix, project, boundaryless

## Common confusions
- **Authority is not the same as power:** authority comes from position; power is the broader ability to influence.
- **Centralization is not formalization:** centralization concerns where decisions are made; formalization concerns rules and standardization.
- **Functional is not divisional:** functional groups specialties; divisional creates self-contained units.

# Chapter 8: Managing Human Resources and Diversity

## Rapid review
Human resource management is the management function concerned with getting, training, motivating, and keeping competent employees. Its purpose is to place the right people in the right jobs at the right time.

### Eight HRM activities
1. Employment planning
2. Recruitment
3. Downsizing
4. Selection
5. Orientation
6. Training and development
7. Performance appraisal
8. Compensation and benefits

The process is influenced by laws, the economy, technology, and social expectations.

### Planning and job analysis
- **Employment planning:** ensures the right number and types of people are available at the right times.
- **Human resource inventory:** records employee education, training, experience, languages, capabilities, and skills.
- **Job analysis:** identifies workflows and the skills and behaviors required.
- **Job description:** states what the job holder does, how, and why.
- **Job specification:** states the minimum knowledge, skills, and attitudes required.

### Recruitment and selection
- **Recruitment:** locating, identifying, and attracting capable applicants.
- **Selection:** predicting which applicants will succeed if hired.
- **Accept error:** hiring an applicant who later performs poorly.
- **Reject error:** rejecting an applicant who would have performed well.
- **Reliability:** a selection device gives consistent results.
- **Validity:** a device has a proven relationship with a relevant job-performance criterion.

### Selection devices
- Written tests
- Performance-simulation tests
- Work sampling
- Assessment centers
- Interviews

Interviews work better when they are structured, standardized, job related, and consistently evaluated. A **realistic job preview** gives both positive and negative information about the job.

### Orientation, training, performance, and compensation
- Orientation reduces anxiety and explains duties, work unit, goals, rules, policies, and procedures.
- Training seeks a relatively permanent improvement in knowledge, skills, or attitudes.
- Training evaluation examines reaction, learning, use on the job, and results.
- A performance management system establishes standards used to evaluate employee performance.
- Compensation should be cost effective and perceived as fair.

### Current HR issues
- Downsizing may create insecurity, guilt, stress, reduced loyalty, and lower effort among survivors.
- Managers must address sexual harassment, work-life needs, contingent workers, workforce diversity, and inclusion.

## Must remember
- Eight HRM activities
- Job description versus specification
- Accept versus reject error
- Reliability versus validity
- Main selection devices
- Orientation and training
- Performance management
- Diversity and inclusion

## Common confusions
- **Recruitment attracts applicants; selection chooses among them.**
- **Reliability means consistency; validity means job relevance.**
- **Job description describes the job; job specification describes the person requirements.**

# Chapter 9: Managing Work Groups and Work Teams

## Rapid review
A group is two or more interacting and interdependent people who come together to achieve specific goals.

### Formal group types
- **Command group:** employees who report directly to a manager.
- **Task group:** people brought together to complete a specific task, often temporarily.
- **Cross-functional team:** people from different specialties.
- **Self-managed team:** an independent group that also performs managerial responsibilities.

### Five stages of group development
1. **Forming:** uncertainty about purpose, structure, and leadership.
2. **Storming:** conflict over control and direction.
3. **Norming:** close relationships, identity, cohesion, and shared expectations.
4. **Performing:** the structure works and effort focuses on the task.
5. **Adjourning:** temporary groups prepare to disband.

### Group behavior concepts
- **Roles:** expected behavior patterns.
- **Norms:** shared standards of acceptable behavior.
- **Conformity:** pressure to align with the group.
- **Status:** prestige or rank.
- **Size:** small groups often complete tasks faster; large groups provide diverse input.
- **Social loafing:** reduced individual effort when responsibility is shared.
- **Cohesiveness:** attraction among members and shared goals.

High cohesiveness improves performance only when the group’s goals support organizational goals.

### Work group versus work team
| Work group | Work team |
|---|---|
| Shares information | Works intensely on a common goal |
| Mainly individual performance | Positive synergy |
| Individual accountability | Individual and mutual accountability |
| Random or varied skills | Complementary skills |

### Team types
- Problem-solving team
- Self-managed work team
- Cross-functional team
- Virtual team

### Effective-team model
- **Context:** resources, leadership and structure, trust, suitable evaluation and rewards.
- **Composition:** abilities, personality, roles, diversity, size, flexibility, preferences.
- **Work design:** autonomy, skill variety, whole task, meaningful impact.
- **Processes:** common purpose, specific goals, efficacy, useful task conflict, low social loafing.

## Must remember
- Formal group types
- Five development stages
- Roles, norms, conformity, status, size, social loafing, cohesiveness
- Group versus team
- Four team types
- Context, composition, work design, processes

## Common confusions
- **A group is not automatically a team.** Teams require common goals, synergy, complementary skills, and mutual accountability.
- **Task conflict may help; relationship conflict is usually harmful.**
- **Cohesiveness alone does not guarantee performance.** Group goals must support the organization.

| Pair | Key distinction |
|---|---|
| Authority / Power | Position-based right / Capacity to influence |
| Centralization / Decentralization | Decisions at upper levels / Decisions or input at lower levels |
| Mechanistic / Organic | Rigid, formal, centralized / Flexible, less formal, decentralized |
| Job description / Job specification | What the job involves / Minimum person requirements |
| Reliability / Validity | Consistency / Relationship with job performance |
| Accept error / Reject error | Hire poor performer / Reject capable applicant |
| Group / Team | Information sharing and individual output / Common goal, synergy, mutual accountability |
