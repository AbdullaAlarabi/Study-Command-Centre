# Chapter 10: Understanding Individual Behavior

## Rapid review
Organizational behavior (OB) is the study of the actions of people at work. Its goals are to explain, predict, and influence behavior.

### Main employee outcomes
- Productivity
- Absenteeism
- Turnover
- Organizational citizenship behavior
- Job satisfaction
- Workplace misbehavior

### Attitudes
An attitude is an evaluative statement about people, objects, or events. Its components are:
- **Cognitive:** beliefs or opinions
- **Affective:** feelings
- **Behavioral:** intention to behave

Important job attitudes are job satisfaction, job involvement, organizational commitment, and employee engagement.

**Cognitive dissonance** is inconsistency between attitudes or between an attitude and behavior. People try to reduce it depending on importance, control, and rewards.

### Personality
- **MBTI dimensions:** Extraversion–Introversion, Sensing–Intuition, Thinking–Feeling, Judging–Perceiving.
- **Big Five:** extraversion, agreeableness, conscientiousness, emotional stability, openness to experience.
- **Emotional intelligence:** self-awareness, self-management, self-motivation, empathy, social skills.
- Other work traits: locus of control, Machiavellianism, self-esteem, self-monitoring, risk propensity.
- Holland’s theory emphasizes fit between personality and job environment.

### Perception and attribution
Perception is how people organize and interpret sensory impressions.

Attribution theory uses:
- **Distinctiveness:** does the person behave differently in other situations?
- **Consensus:** do others behave similarly?
- **Consistency:** does the person behave this way repeatedly?

Biases:
- **Fundamental attribution error:** overestimate internal causes and underestimate external causes when judging others.
- **Self-serving bias:** credit success internally and blame failure externally.
- **Selective perception, assumed similarity, stereotyping, halo effect** are perceptual shortcuts.

### Learning and behavior shaping
- **Operant conditioning:** behavior is influenced by consequences.
- **Social learning:** people learn through observation and direct experience; it requires attention, retention, motor reproduction, and reinforcement.
- Behavior shaping: positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement, punishment, extinction.

## Must remember
- OB goals and six outcomes
- Attitude components and job attitudes
- Big Five and five EI dimensions
- Attribution factors and biases
- Four perceptual shortcuts
- Four behavior-shaping methods

# Chapter 11: Motivating and Rewarding Employees

## Rapid review
Motivation is the process by which effort is energized, directed, and sustained toward a goal. Its elements are **energy, direction, and persistence**.

### Early theories
- **Maslow:** physiological, safety, social, esteem, self-actualization. A satisfied need loses motivating power.
- **Theory X:** workers dislike work, avoid responsibility, and need control.
- **Theory Y:** workers can enjoy work, accept responsibility, and use self-direction.
- **Herzberg:** motivators create satisfaction; hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction.
- **McClelland:** needs for achievement, power, and affiliation.

### Goal-setting theory
- Specific goals improve performance.
- Difficult goals often produce higher performance when accepted.
- Feedback identifies the gap between actual and desired results.
- Goal commitment, self-efficacy, and culture affect results.

### Job characteristics model
Five dimensions:
1. Skill variety
2. Task identity
3. Task significance
4. Autonomy
5. Feedback

The first three create meaningfulness; autonomy creates responsibility; feedback gives knowledge of results. Job enrichment adds planning and evaluation responsibility.

### Equity theory
Employees compare their outcomes-to-inputs ratio with a referent.
- **Distributive justice:** fairness of reward amounts.
- **Procedural justice:** fairness of the process used to allocate rewards.

### Expectancy theory
- **Expectancy:** effort will lead to performance.
- **Instrumentality:** performance will lead to reward.
- **Valence:** the reward is valuable.

All three links must be strong for high motivation.

### Current practices
Open-book management, employee recognition, pay-for-performance, clear roles, open communication, employee input, shared goals, community, and development opportunities.

## Must remember
- Energy, direction, persistence
- Maslow, X/Y, Herzberg, McClelland
- Goal-setting conditions
- Five job dimensions
- Equity and justice
- Expectancy, instrumentality, valence

# Chapter 12: Understanding Leadership

## Rapid review
A leader can influence others and has managerial authority. Leadership is the process of leading a group and influencing it to achieve goals.

### Trait and behavioral theories
Seven traits associated with leadership:
- Drive
- Desire to lead
- Honesty and integrity
- Self-confidence
- Intelligence
- Job-relevant knowledge
- Extraversion

Behavioral approaches:
- Iowa: democratic, autocratic, laissez-faire
- Ohio State: consideration and initiating structure
- Michigan: employee oriented and production oriented
- Managerial Grid: concern for people and concern for production; 9,9 is high-high

### Four contingency theories
1. **Fiedler:** style is task or relationship oriented; situation depends on leader-member relations, task structure, and position power.
2. **Situational leadership:** style depends on follower readiness. Telling, selling, participating, delegating.
3. **Leader-participation model:** decision tree identifies suitable employee participation.
4. **Path-goal theory:** leader clarifies the path, removes obstacles, and uses directive, supportive, participative, or achievement-oriented behavior.

### Modern leadership
- **LMX:** leaders develop in-groups and out-groups.
- **Transactional:** motivates through exchanges and rewards.
- **Transformational:** inspires extraordinary outcomes and challenges old assumptions.
- **Charismatic:** enthusiastic, self-confident, visionary, risk accepting.
- **Visionary:** creates and communicates a realistic and attractive future.

### Teams, empowerment, culture, EI, and trust
- Team leaders manage the external boundary and facilitate team processes.
- Empowerment increases employee decision discretion.
- Emotional intelligence supports effective leadership.
- Trust is belief in a leader’s integrity, character, and ability.
- Five trust dimensions: integrity, competence, consistency, loyalty, openness.

## Must remember
- Seven traits
- Four early behavioral approaches
- Four contingency theories
- Fiedler’s three variables
- Four situational styles and readiness levels
- Four path-goal behaviors
- Transactional versus transformational
- Five trust dimensions

**Frame:** define motivation, then summarize Maslow, X/Y, Herzberg, and McClelland with their main concepts.

**Frame:** outcomes/input comparison and justice; then expectancy, instrumentality, valence.

## 6. Describe the four contingency leadership theories.
**Frame:** Fiedler, situational leadership, leader-participation, path-goal; state what each uses to match leadership to the situation.

## 7. Compare transactional and transformational leadership and explain trust.
**Frame:** define both styles, give contrasts, define trust, list five dimensions, explain how leaders build it.
